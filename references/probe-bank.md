# Probe Bank

The seller says something about the deal. That tell points at the gap. You ask one question to confirm it, then you and the rep pick a move that advances the deal. You are not interrogating the rep about what they are missing. You are working the deal forward with them.

So each scenario has two parts:
- **The probe.** One question to confirm the gap is real.
- **Plays to advance.** A menu of moves to run if it is. Each scenario marks one play as **Start here**. That is the keystone, the highest-leverage move, usually the one that puts a buyer decision in motion. Reach for it first, then add one more if it helps. Do not run the whole menu. The meeting-getting plays beat the sending plays, because the method is about buyer decisions, not seller activity.

The voice is partner, not professor. Plays are borrowed and adapted from MEDDPICC, Gap Selling (Keenan), Command of the Message (Force Management), and the Gong and Clari risk-coaching practice.

## "They love it, it's going great"

**Gap:** no risk surfaced, happy ears.
**Probe:** "If this deal dies in 60 days, what killed it?"

**Plays to advance:**
- **Start here:** Lock the next step on the buyer's calendar before the current call ends, so enthusiasm turns into a committed date.
- Send a mutual close plan the buyer edits with you. What they change tells you what is real.
- Ask the buyer to bring in one more stakeholder, with a reason built for that person's own metric so the ask lands. A warm buyer says yes, a polite one stalls, and either way you learn where you stand.
- Set up a small exec-to-exec touch to validate the deal from the top.
- Get one proof of intent this week: a signed order of magnitude, a security review kicked off, a date held.

## Only ever names one contact

**Gap:** single-threaded.
**Probe:** "Who else has to nod on this, and who inside their org loses if it ships?"

**Plays to advance:**
- **Start here:** Map the buying committee together, then book one new-stakeholder meeting this week.
- Use the existing contact to multi-thread. Ask them to introduce the next person up or across.
- Bring your own exec in to open a peer door the rep cannot open alone.
- Build a role-specific reason for each new persona, tied to the metric they are measured on, so the outreach lands.
- Find the person who loses if this ships and get in front of them before they surface as a blocker.

## "They need to get budget approved"

**Gap:** decision process is a black box.
**Probe:** "Walk me through the exact approval path. Who signs, and what do they need to see to sign?"

**Plays to advance:**
- **Start here:** Build a mutual action plan backward from the signature date, with their internal steps named, not just ours.
- Have the champion walk you through the real approval path, including who has killed deals like this before.
- Start the paper early. Get procurement and legal moving in parallel instead of in sequence.
- Have the champion model the business case in the approver's own language, usually a payback period or an ROI threshold the approver already uses.
- Get a meeting with the economic buyer directly, rather than relaying through a user.
- Offer a phased or pilot start to lower the approval bar and create momentum.

## Names a champion warmly

**Gap:** the champion may be a friend, not power.
**Probe:** "Will they argue for us in a room we are not in? What have they actually spent to back us?"

**Plays to advance:**
- **Start here:** Test them with a small ask, like setting up a meeting, and watch whether they deliver. That is the cheapest read on whether they have power.
- Give the champion a forwardable one-page business case so selling us costs them less effort.
- Role-play their internal pitch with them, so they walk into their meeting ready.
- Hand them a win they can claim internally, tied to what they personally get if this closes, since no one fights for you without a personal stake.
- Build a second champion so the deal does not rest on one relationship.

## Deal is "stuck" or "slipping"

**Gap:** no compelling event.
**Probe:** "What changes for them if they do nothing? If the honest answer is nothing, why is this in our pipe?"

**Plays to advance:**
- **Start here:** Tie the deal to a date that already matters to them: a fiscal cycle, a launch, a renewal, a contract expiry.
- Put a number on the cost of delay per month, so doing nothing has a price.
- If there is genuine scarcity, like real implementation capacity or a launch slot that actually closes, use it. Do not invent a deadline, because sophisticated buyers see through a fake one and it costs you trust.
- Align the close to their planning cycle so the timing works for them, not just for you.
- If no event exists and none can be built, move the deal down and stop spending the team's time on it.

## Suddenly needs a discount

**Gap:** value was never established, or procurement has entered.
**Probe:** "What did we show them they lose at the lower number? What is our 2x recommendation?"

**Plays to advance:**
- **Start here:** Diagnose the ask before you react. A discount request late in the cycle is often a procurement process gate, which is a buying signal, not a value failure. Treat the two differently.
- If value is the gap, re-anchor on the cost of inaction before talking price at all.
- Bring the 2x recommendation: what they miss if they only buy what they asked for.
- Build a simple ROI model using the buyer's own numbers, so the value is theirs not yours.
- Trade every concession. No price move without term, scope, a reference, or a faster close in return.
- Bring in a reference customer who paid full value and got the return.

## "We sent the proposal"

**Gap:** a seller task is standing in for buyer progress.
**Probe:** "What decision does the proposal let them make? Who reads it, and what do they do next because of it?"

**Plays to advance:**
- **Start here:** Get on the calendar to present it to the decision-maker. If they will not take that meeting, the proposal went out too early.
- Walk it through live rather than emailing it, so you control the framing and hear the reaction.
- Pre-wire it with the champion before it reaches the committee, so there are no surprises in the room.
- Attach a mutual action plan so the proposal has a path, not just a price.

## Talks only about our product and features

**Gap:** no buyer decision in view.
**Probe:** "What decision is the buyer actually trying to make right now? What are they stuck on?"

**Plays to advance:**
- **Start here:** Shift the conversation to the buyer's business outcome and the metric they are measured on.
- Run another round of discovery before any more pitching.
- Map our capability to their decision, so the deal is framed in their terms.
- Get the rep to write the deal from the buyer's point of view, which surfaces what they do not yet know.

## Legal, security, or procurement just entered

**Gap:** late-stage process risk, where the deal is sold but the paper can still stall it for weeks.
**Probe:** "What is their actual procurement and security path from here, and what has stalled deals at this stage before?"

**Plays to advance:**
- **Start here:** Get your security and legal pre-pack ready before they ask, so review does not add weeks.
- Run redlines in parallel, not in sequence, and get your legal and theirs talking directly.
- Have the champion walk the MSA and the security questionnaire internally so it does not sit in a queue.
- Map the remaining steps onto the mutual action plan with named owners and dates on their side.
- Find out who in procurement or security can say no, and get ahead of their objection before it surfaces.

## A competitor or incumbent is in the deal

**Gap:** differentiation not established, the buyer cannot say why us.
**Probe:** "Why us over what they have or who else they are looking at, in the buyer's own words? If they cannot say, we have not differentiated."

**Plays to advance:**
- **Start here:** Establish why change, why now, why us in the buyer's language, and get the buyer to say it back.
- Tie the decision criteria to where we are strong and the competitor is weak, so the criteria do the selling.
- Get the champion to own our differentiation internally so it survives the room we are not in.
- If we are displacing an incumbent, surface the real cost of the switch and the cost of staying, so the buyer weighs both honestly.
- Reframe the decision criteria rather than trashing the competitor.

## The champion went quiet

**Gap:** the champion has disengaged, the slow-death pattern.
**Probe:** "When did they last move something forward for us, and what changed? Silence usually means a priority shift or a problem they have not told us about."

**Plays to advance:**
- **Start here:** Reach the champion with a reason to re-engage that serves them, like new information or a win they can use, not a check-in.
- Multi-thread now as insurance so the deal does not rest on one quiet contact.
- Go to a second contact to learn what changed inside their org.
- Give the champion an easy, specific next step rather than an open-ended ask.
- If the silence holds, name it honestly and decide whether the deal is still real.

## "It's a big logo, we have to win it"

**Gap:** surface gravity is standing in for real qualification.
**Probe:** "Strip the logo. On the merits, is this a deal we win, and is it one we should even keep?"

**Plays to advance:**
- **Start here:** Qualify on the merits with the gap map, ignoring the brand.
- Set a kept-or-killed checkpoint with a date and clear criteria, so the logo earns its place and cannot bleed time slowly.
- If it is weak, put it on break-up watch rather than letting it absorb the team's time.
- If it is real, resource it like a flagship and bring exec air cover early.

## The two signature moves

Two plays that carry the method. Reach for them often.

- **The 2x recommendation.** Show the buyer what they miss if you just give them what they asked for. Moves the rep from order-taker to advisor and builds the value case that defends price.
- **The internal install.** Coach the buyer's own internal sale. Help their advocate get budget approved, get the financial approver what they need, and clear the path inside their org. This is where enterprise deals are won.

## Form

Confirm the gap with one probe, then move to a play. Spend the meeting on what to do, not on a longer list of what to figure out. Let the rep own the move so the thinking is theirs and it carries to the next deal.

A few moves to avoid:
- Do not run the whole menu. One or two real moves beat five half-moves.
- Do not let a low-friction send stand in for the meeting that actually moves the decision.
- Do not manufacture a fake deadline. Use urgency only when the scarcity is real.
- Do not negotiate price before the deal is confirmed real and dated.
- Do not multi-thread by spraying outreach. Every new contact gets a reason built for them.
